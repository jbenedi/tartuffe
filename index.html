TARTUFFE: A PLAY IN THE PACIFIC NORTH WEST
By James Benedict

The play I have chosen for this production concept is Tartuffe by Molière, first performed in 1664. Tartuffe is a funny comedy about hypocrisy, deception, and trusting the wrong people. The play is about Tartuffe, a con artist pretending to be a deep spiritual man who tricks Orgon, a wealthy and gullible man, into trusting him completely and almost to the family’s downfall. The play’s themes of fake appearances and misplaced trust are still very relevant today.

In my concept, Tartuffe is set in a small, rainy Pacific Northwest town in the 1990s, a time and place where grunge music, flannel shirts, and coffee shop culture were and still are everywhere, but people were also questioning authority and societal norms. It fits pretty well with the play’s themes of appearances versus reality. The setting helps create a moody and almost dreary world where Tartuffe’s false image of virtue is opposite to most of the grounded people around him.

In this version, Orgon is a naïve business startup owner looking for spiritual guidance in a world that feels chaotic and uncertain. Tartuffe is a fake new age guru or influencer, manipulating Orgon’s need for meaning and guilt over his privilege. The rest of the family sees through Tartuffe, but they have to figure out how to convince Orgon. The production will use the gray, rainy atmosphere of the Pacific Northwest, with cozy but worn-out flannel, thrifted furniture, and misty forests, to highlight the contrast between what’s real and what’s fake in the small-town. We will see the warm interior of the house they live in, while the outside is gloomy and dark hinting at something bad lurking.

RELIGIOUS HYP OCRISY IN THE 17TH CENTURY


Religious hypocrisy was a big issue in 17th century France, since the Catholic Church had a lot of power over society and morality. Molière’s Tartuffe criticized people who used religion for personal gain, making it so controversial that the play was banned after its first performance. Tartuffe’s false wisdom and manipulation showed the dangers of blind faith to people of authority.

COMEDY INCRI TIQUE


Molière’s comedies used satire and farce to criticize society’s flaws while also entertaining watchers. In Tartuffe, the humor comes from the exaggerated characters, comedic dialogue, and physical comedy. This combination of criticism and entertainment let Molière address serious issues without losing his audience.


My Costumes

Physical comedy could have Tartuffe awkwardly fumbling with a cassette tape or tripping over his flowing robes during a yoga session.

Using 1990s humor and cultural references makes the comedy more relatable for modern audiences while preserving Molière’s satire. This concept would help highlight how humor is still a powerful tool for critisizing societal norms.
GREEK STAGE DESIGN


In Ancient Greek Theatre, simple stage designs were used and performed in open-air amphitheaters. The focus was usually on the actors and the story as opposed to on the elaborate sets and effects. The simplicity allowed the audience to concentrate on what was being said and the action being done.

GREEK MASKS


In Ancient Greek Theatre, masks were very often used in most performances. Masks allowed actors to portray many different characters and help to make it clear to the audience that they might have been expressing a certain emotion. Big Greek Theaters made it difficult for all audience members to understand the action on stage, but masks allowed emotions and expressions to be over-dramatized so that people sitting far away could easily see and understand the way each character was being portrayed.


My Masks

In my production, masks would be used on the chorus in a few different forms. Some would have daft-punk style LED masks that could show different emotions, while others would have more basic masks like gas masks or goggles. In contrast, the main more wealthy characters would wear simpler things, like high-tech fancy glasses, or slim goggles.


These masks would all help to tie together the message of the state of society at the time, like all the other parts of this play’s design. The chorus’ masks would show futuristic technology while also giving some context to maybe the air quality of the place they live, since some of them wear gas masks. The contrast of the wealthy’s glasses, high tech gear, and lack of things like gas masks would show their wealth and how they might not be as much a victim to poor air quality and bad living conditions.
